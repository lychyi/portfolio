My team was tasked to take over a small form-based software that was responsible for helping researchers customize their Illumina products. We were challenged to give it a fresh and efficient new design while refactoring the difficult to maintain, monolithic code-base behind it. As the lead designer and front end developer, I was responsible for designing a brand new web application for it from the ground up.

The microarray assay has been a core technology of Illumina for nearly a 15 years. It has been utilized to support some of the most well-known genomics entities out there like 23 and Me and Ancestry.com. Creating customized versions of this assay is still the heart of genomic research in many areas today.

While a lot of Illumina's products innovate quickly, the supporting software often lags behind. ADT has been successful but largely untouched throughout the years other than the occasional bugfix. In 2016, we were given the green light to redesign ADT to accommodate a new product launch.

What is (was) ADT?

ADT would allow a researcher to submit a comma-separated value (CSV) file that inquires about specific genomic targets (or locations) for a particular species. This would be the foundation for an experiment they could run over and over to do in-depth analysis on a sample of DNA. For example, if an oncologist wanted to do research on lung cancer, they could create an experiment that tests for the existence of genetic markers that would give them scientific insight through DNA.

It would then do basic validation of the CSV file and then crunch through it with a complex algroithm. Certain areas of the genome are harder to analyze than others so one of ADT's main jobs was to ensure that the researcher knows the likelihood of success for each target. Once everything was verified to their liking, Illumina could then create a custom microarray for the researcher (we call it a 'beadchip') so that they could do their study as efficiently as possible.

Product Goals

Our new software had to accomplish a few goals:

  1. Be similar to the way ADT functions currently as to not alienate current users familiar with it
  2. Have a similar look, feel, & experience with DesignStudio, another custom product platform, so that users can have a cohesive experience designing any custom product at Illumina
  3. Be a modernized software product to parallel a new, modernized microarray product

Code from scratch

ADT had been in use for at least a decade and the time had come to start from scratch. Modern web technologies were much improved. At the time, Angular was about to release version 1.5 and the popular framework was at its most mature state. The supporting tools had gotten so good, applications practically bundled and deployed themselves (of course you have to go through a brutal rite-of-passage called Webpack 'configuration'). Moreover, the user interface needed a facelift and we could easily solve common painpoints of ADT just by re-thinking the workflow from start to finish.

The journey took a about 6 months but ultimately, the results were well worth the challenges. We evolved the platform into an HTML forms-based web page to an elegant, single-page Angular application.

On the performance side, we improved pageload times by optimizing the front end vendor files using Webpack. We automated the deployment to various environments using Github, Team City, and Octopus Deploy.

Component-driven Design

By simply addressing the design elements from an atomic level and then assembling upwards, more complex interfaces can take shape with predictable results.

Component libraries pay for themselves over time

For just about any project that I start, I spend time to craft a library of quality 'atoms' that are used to prototype user interfaces with Goldilocks-fidelity (not too high, not too low). I do this for two reasons:

  1. User feedback on believable prototypes are generally more authentic compared to when users know they're just testing a 'prototype'.
  2. Validated features and screens can easily be passed onto developers with little to no modifications since they are visually true to the final design.

Don't get me wrong, I love pen and paper and lo-fidelity mockups for quick ideation when time is at a premium, but those are a one-time use effort. Having my own well-crafted component library is something that has multiple uses and gives value throughout the lifecycle of a project. By simply addressing the design elements from an atomic level and then assembling upwards, more complex interfaces can take shape with predictable results.

DesignStudio's Component library

Sketch is built for the job

Sketch has made it elegant to build component libraries with their symbols feature. It allows easy reuse of common user interface elements across artboards and fits perfectly with a component-driven design process. Ultimately, it makes the design process fast, often times turning ideas into prototypes within the same day (or hour).

One vastly underrated feature of Sketch is the ecosystem of plugins that the community has built around it. Plugins like Runner and Craft really show how creative you can get with them and how a bit of code can save designers hours every single day. These plugins also help keep my designs component-driven. They force me to be conscious about building useful, re-usable components and often times, this translates into re-usable CSS styles and JavaScript code too.
